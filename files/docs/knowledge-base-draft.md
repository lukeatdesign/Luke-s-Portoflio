## 3. Work Process & Methodology

**Q: What does your design process look like?**
A: I follow the Double Diamond — diverge to understand the full problem space, then converge on a specific solution. In practice that means: research (user interviews, contextual inquiry, competitive analysis), define (problem statement, user journey mapping, information architecture), ideate (design studio sessions, wireframes, UX Dissect sessions with PO and devs), prototype (hi-fi interactive prototypes in Figma), and test (usability testing, acceptance testing). Design typically runs one sprint ahead of development.

**Q: How do you work with developers?**
A: Very closely. I write developer tickets myself — user stories and acceptance criteria — and conduct acceptance tests when features are built to verify they match the design intent. I discuss technical feasibility early in the design process rather than handing off to find out something isn't buildable. I run "UX Dissect" sessions during wireframing where devs and PMs can flag constraints before we go hi-fi. I also maintain design notes alongside prototypes so the intended behaviour is never ambiguous.

**Q: How do you handle tight deadlines?**
A: "Slow is smooth, and smooth is fast." My approach is to maintain quality even under pressure — specifically by making everything reusable. If I have two days, I still build components and variants properly. That preparation is what lets the team move fast later. The Initia MultiSig project is a good example: we delivered high-quality mockups for a complex multi-signature wallet within one week because the design system was solid and I knew how to work within it.

**Q: What does a design system mean to you?**
A: A design system is what lets a team scale quality. I always build in Atomic Design — components, variants, states — organised so a new designer can onboard in about a week. I align design tokens with developer needs specifically so that implementation doesn't drift from design intent. On Celatone and InitiaScan, a well-maintained design system was what allowed us to keep adding features for two years without accruing massive design debt.

**Q: Do you do usability testing?**
A: Yes, and I believe in it strongly. Celatone's 94% task completion rate came from iterative usability testing during development. On Markr, we measured a 30% higher task accomplishment rate and 40% reduction in time with the revamped interface. The honest lesson I've also learned is that when I've designed under deadline without testing, I've been wrong in ways I couldn't predict — "we cannot design and test on our own" is something I now take seriously.

**Q: How do you communicate design decisions to stakeholders?**
A: Design demos during delivery, detailed design notes in Figma, and active listening in meetings. I try to frame decisions in terms of business goals and user needs rather than aesthetic preference. I've worked at companies where UX wasn't well understood, and part of my job was educating stakeholders — at RentSpree, I spent time promoting UX maturity across the company and helped restructure the product design team.

**Q: How do you handle design feedback and iteration?**
A: With curiousity rather than defensiveness. I've worked in environments without a design manager, which means I've had to develop my own critical eye and actively seek feedback. I run structured dissection sessions specifically to surface concerns from developers and PMs before they become expensive problems.

**Q: What is the Double Diamond and how do you apply it?**
A: It's a design process framework from the British Design Council: two diamonds representing diverge-converge cycles. First diamond: open up to understand the full problem space, then converge to define the specific problem. Second diamond: ideate widely, then converge on the solution. I find it especially useful on complex projects where there's a temptation to jump to solutions — it forces proper problem definition before the design work starts.

**Q: How do you handle projects where technical constraints limit your design vision?**
A: I try to understand the constraint deeply first — often what seems like a limitation has a workaround, or the constraint itself suggests a different, better design. On Vertical Protocol, the smart contract couldn't calculate borrowing maximums dynamically, which broke the slider constraint I'd designed. We adapted — removing the slider, adding real-time feedback displays — and it still worked. The constraint became a design driver rather than just a problem.

### Celatone (2022–2024)

**Q: What is Celatone?**
A: Celatone is a smart contract developer tool and block explorer for CosmWasm-supported blockchain networks. **Q: What were the usability test results for Celatone?**
A: 94% average task completion rate across 46 subtasks with 5 participants. Average satisfaction rating of 4.48 out of 5 (individual scores: 4.75, 4.375, 4.3, 4.5). Error rate was 6% — 3 out of 46 subtasks not completed.

**Q: What was your role on Celatone?**

**Q: What led to the Celatone acquisition?**

(2) Research time reduces implementation cost by 100x. From Ladder: we rushed the research phase and built a product without solid product-market fit. Those are expensive lessons I carry into every project now.

**Q: What's a work situation you've found genuinely difficult?**
A: Working in companies where UX was not understood or valued, and having to advocate for the process while also trying to deliver quality work within that same process. It's gotten easier because I've gotten better at explaining the "why" in business terms rather than design terms.

**Q: What does success look like for a product to you?**
A: Users can accomplish their goals without frustration, without needing to think about the interface. Celatone's 94% task completion rate isn't just a metric — it means developers could actually do their job using the tool we built. That's the outcome I optimise for.

The blockchain chapter was where I spent my most recent years, but I view every project through the lens of problem-fit and market-fit regardless of the industry. The UX challenges in complex financial products or developer tools are really just harder versions of universal problems: discoverability, cognitive load, error prevention, and task completion.

**Q: What's unique about designing for DeFi products?**
A: DeFi users are making real financial decisions with real money, often under emotional pressure (greed/fear). The terminology is domain-specific and unfamiliar to newcomers. Errors — like misconfiguring a leverage position — can be irreversible and costly. This raises the stakes for clarity, error prevention, and proactive help significantly compared to most consumer products. I've approached it by: understanding the financial mechanics deeply enough to explain them clearly, using visual hierarchy to surface the most critical decision points, and adding proactive help tips and constraints that prevent common errors.

**Q: What was your experience with the Terra ecosystem collapse?**
A: Two of my projects — Nebula Protocol and Vertical Protocol — were suspended when Terra's ecosystem failed (UST lost its $1 peg, triggering LUNA hyperinflation). It was a real reminder that even well-designed products can be taken out by infrastructure risk. Both projects were solid design work; the failure was entirely external. The lesson I carry is about ecosystem risk in early-stage blockchain projects and why market/ecosystem validation matters alongside product-market fit.

## 9. Additional Project Details

**Q: Which project are you most proud of?**
A: Celatone, without hesitation. The 94% task completion rate in usability testing confirmed the design was working, not just looking good. And the two years of iterative maintenance taught me as much as the initial build.

**Q: Which project taught you the most?**
A: Ladder — because it failed in the market, not because the design was bad. Three years of work on a product that became inactive because we didn't validate product-market fit properly. That's the most expensive lesson in my career and the one I apply most consistently now: research time isn't overhead, it's investment.

**Q: Can you describe a time you influenced a company's UX culture?**
A: At RentSpree in 2021. Most of my prior roles involved companies with little UX awareness. RentSpree was more sophisticated — executives cared about UX — but the team structure wasn't set up to scale. In my last three months I was specifically tasked with promoting UX maturity: restructuring the product design team, introducing new positions and workflows, and serving as design reviewer to ensure interaction consistency. It's the kind of work I find genuinely meaningful.

**Q: Have you worked solo or with design teams?**
A: Both. Several early projects (Ladder, Alpha Homora V2) I was the only designer. Celatone, InitiaScan, Initia MultiSig, and Weave CLI all had two designers. RentSpree had two product designers. The team composition significantly changes how you work — solo means you own the entire visual and UX quality bar yourself, team means you also have to create systems and design standards that another person can work within.

**Q: What's your experience with design handoffs?**
A: Handoff is something I take seriously as a discipline, not just a step. I maintain design systems in Atomic Design so developers always have components with all states defined. I write design notes in Figma to document intended behaviour. I align design tokens with development tokens to prevent drift. I conduct acceptance tests after implementation to close the loop. The consequence of bad handoffs isn't just "things look wrong" — on financial or developer tools, it can mean features that behave incorrectly in production.

**Q: What's the most complex information architecture problem you've solved?**
A: Celatone's sitemap design. Smart contract developers have a very specific workflow: upload WASM file → instantiate → query → execute → manage. Previous tools scattered these steps across multiple apps. Designing a sitemap that made the full workflow visible, accessible, and sequential — while also exposing block explorer features for more general users — required a lot of thinking about mental models and user goals before anything was sketched.

**Q: Have you designed for data visualisation?**
A: Yes, primarily on Ladder. I designed dashboards using information foraging theory so analysts could see keyword, channel, and sentiment overviews before drilling into details. The challenge was that data visualisation for social analytics doesn't have established best practices the way, say, finance dashboards do — I had to reason from first principles about what insights analysts actually needed and in what order.

**Q: What's your experience with surveys and quantitative research?**
A: I used Hubspot for survey design and Power BI for visualising results on the RentSpree Rent Payment project. The team had assumptions about user behaviour that hadn't been validated, and we launched a survey to get clear directional data before committing to specific design decisions. I'm comfortable with mixed-methods — knowing when to use quant vs qual, and how to let each inform the other.

**Q: Have you worked with product managers?**
A: Yes, extensively. Most of my projects involved a PM or Product Owner. I believe strongly in the designer-PM relationship as a collaborative peer relationship, not a brief-taking one. I contribute to user story definition, push back on scope when user needs are being shortcut, and try to keep research findings in front of the product strategy conversation rather than treating them as pre-design homework only.

**Q: What industries have you worked in?**
A: Sports media (content/digital strategy), social analytics (Backyard/Ladder), data tooling (Backyard/Markr), US rental tech (RentSpree), and blockchain/Web3 (Alpha Finance Lab, Alles Labs, Initia Labs). The range is fairly broad, but the last 3.5+ years have been deep in blockchain — DeFi products and developer tools.

**Q: How do you stay current with design trends and tools?**
A: Mostly through structured learning (IDF courses, Uxcel), reading books (I track these on Goodreads), and community — I've been part of the Thai UX community as a speaker and writer, which keeps me connected to what practitioners are actually working through. Writing and teaching also forces currency: you can't teach usability principles credibly if you're not applying and testing them in your own work.
